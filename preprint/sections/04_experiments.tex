\section{Experiments}\label{sec:experiments}

The experiments follow the paper's two-sided claim.
\begin{itemize}
  \item First, even under pure covariate shift, worst-group audits can create false subgroup alarms when local weighted support collapses.
  \item Second, real hidden subgroup failures still exist, so the right response is not to stop auditing subgroups but to audit them with local overlap diagnostics.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Experiment 1: false alarms under pure covariate shift}

The main experimental study is new and is saved in \path{preprint/research/local_ess_study.py}.
It uses \path{src/shiftstat/datasets/synthetic.py} and intentionally chooses the cleanest possible setting: the data are generated under pure covariate shift, and the prediction score is the true conditional probability. In other words,
\[
Y\given X \sim \mathrm{Bernoulli}(\score(X))
\]
under both source and target. The model is therefore calibrated by construction.

This design removes the usual ambiguity. If a worst-group audit reports a large subgroup-bin calibration gap in this experiment, that is a false alarm caused by weighted finite-sample noise.

The audit family is intentionally richer than a single pre-specified slice. We use:
\begin{itemize}
  \item threshold groups based on \(x_0,x_1,x_2\);
  \item negative-tail and positive-tail slices;
  \item pairwise positive-tail intersections;
  \item seven score bins.
\end{itemize}

We run the study in two modes:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Oracle weights:} exact density-ratio weights from the Gaussian covariate-shift generator, over \(200\) random seeds.
  \item \textbf{Learned weights:} logistic density-ratio estimation through the library's \texttt{ImportanceWeighter}, over \(50\) random seeds at the middle shift severities.
\end{itemize}

The main empirical result is simple: as shift grows, lower-tail local ESS collapses much faster than global ESS, and false worst-group alarms become common even though the true worst-group error is zero.

The saved study also reports correlation diagnostics. Under oracle weights, the pooled correlation between \(\log(1+{\ess}_{b,g})\) and the absolute subgroup-bin normalized gap is about \(-0.49\), whereas the corresponding pooled correlation for global ESS is only about \(-0.30\). Within each fixed shift level, lower-tail local ESS also predicts worst-group noise more strongly than global ESS. This is the empirical analogue of \cref{cor:false-alarm}.

Since there is no true subgroup miscalibration in the data-generating process, the only reason for a large worst-group estimate is statistical instability. The experiment therefore makes the false-alarm mechanism directly visible.

\subsection{Experiment 2: real hidden failures still occur}

The local-ESS study explains false alarms. It does not remove the need for subgroup auditing. To show that, we keep the repository's subgroup-degradation benchmarks as a second experiment.

These scenarios come from \path{src/shiftstat/datasets/audit_synthetic.py}. Here the target process is changed in a subgroup-specific way, so the subgroup failures are real rather than noise-driven. Two representative cases are:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Masked subgroup shift:} worst-group ECE gap \(\approx 0.182\).
  \item \textbf{Minority subgroup degradation:} worst-group ECE gap \(\approx 0.217\).
\end{itemize}

Taken together, the two experiments say exactly what the theory says.
\begin{itemize}
  \item Worst-group auditing is necessary because aggregate metrics can hide real subgroup failures.
  \item Worst-group auditing is not self-authenticating because local overlap collapse can also create false subgroup alarms.
\end{itemize}

The practical implication is therefore simple: whenever a worst-group audit is reported under covariate shift, the report should also include local support diagnostics such as subgroup-bin effective sample sizes or lower-tail summaries of those quantities.
