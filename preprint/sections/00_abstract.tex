Worst-group reliability is increasingly used to audit models under deployment shift. The usual intuition is simple: if the model fails badly on one subgroup, an average metric can hide that failure. This paper shows that there is a second problem. Under covariate shift, a worst-group audit can also report a subgroup failure that is not real, because the weighted information available for that subgroup may collapse locally even when the global weighted sample still looks healthy.

We study binary prediction under covariate shift and write target subgroup-bin calibration gaps as importance-weighted source quantities. We then identify the right variance scale for these estimators. For each score bin and subgroup, the conditional variance of the self-normalized weighted residual estimator is governed by a subgroup-bin local effective sample size, not by the usual global effective sample size alone. This yields a finite-sample false-alarm bound for worst-group audits in terms of the minimum local effective sample size across the audited cells. We also give a construction showing that global effective sample size can remain large while one subgroup-bin cell has effective sample size one.

Experiments in the repository support the same story. In a pure covariate-shift benchmark where the model is calibrated by construction, lower-tail local effective sample size collapses much faster than global effective sample size, and false worst-group alarms become common. The same pattern persists with learned logistic density-ratio weights. We also retain subgroup-degradation benchmarks showing that real hidden failures do exist in practice. The paper's message is therefore not to avoid worst-group auditing, but to pair it with local overlap diagnostics. Without that extra check, the audit can confuse statistical noise with deployment failure.
